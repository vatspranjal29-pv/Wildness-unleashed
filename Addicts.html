Bloody Addicts

The day I realized I was one of them — a reflection on attention, fear, and becoming.

Bloody Addicts

Part I of a longer reflection • by Pranjal Vats

I won’t start with pleasantries. Those are distractions. And this is a story about distractions.

I come from a middle-class family where dreams are introduced early, but comfort is postponed indefinitely. Growth wasn’t optional—it was assumed. If I wanted a better life, I would have to earn it slowly, deliberately.

That’s enough introduction. Let’s begin.

A Day That Didn’t Matter — Until It Did

Nothing remarkable happened that day. No milestones. No achievements worth mentioning.

On my way back from coaching, I was stuck in traffic—the kind that drains you without moving an inch. My phone battery was low, so for once, I didn’t reach for it. I kept it aside, almost like saving it for an emergency I couldn’t define.

Out of boredom, I looked outside. That’s when I saw them.

A World Full of Addicts

Not the kind we usually imagine. No needles. No dark rooms.

These addicts were well-dressed, seated comfortably in cars, buses, autos. Heads bent. Fingers scrolling. Eyes glazed.

Everyone was somewhere else. Present physically. Absent mentally.

A car beside me caught my attention. Seven people inside. Seven screens. Not one conversation.

Two children glued to a game. A teenage girl staring endlessly at a photograph of an actor. A mother scrolling in circles. A father sealed behind headphones. A grandfather recording a vlog. And the driver—texting.

The Moment That Changed Everything

The discomfort wasn’t about what I saw outside. It was about what I recognized inside.

I wasn’t just watching the addicts. I was one of them.

That quiet realization—when observation turned inward—is where this story truly begins.

Closing Reflection

Addiction doesn’t always destroy lives. Sometimes it simply steals attention.

And attention, once lost, quietly takes meaning with it.
