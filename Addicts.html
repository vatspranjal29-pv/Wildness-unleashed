Bloody Addicts

The day I realized I was one of them — a reflection on attention, fear, and becoming.

Part I: Bloody Addicts

A reflection on distraction, attention, and self-recognition • Pranjal Vats

I won’t start with pleasantries. Those are distractions—and this chapter is about distractions.

My name is Pranjal. I come from a very supportive family, and a society where dreams are taught early and comfort is postponed indefinitely. I grew up knowing—almost instinctively—that growth wasn’t optional. If I wanted a better life for my family and myself, I would have to earn it, inch by inch.

That’s enough of an introduction. Let’s begin.

Chapter 1: The Accepted Escape

Today wasn’t extraordinary. No milestones. No achievements worth announcing.

And yet, I learned something unsettling—something that stayed with me long after the day ended.

On my way back from CLAT coaching, I was stuck in traffic. The kind that doesn’t move but still exhausts you. My phone battery was low, so for once, I didn’t reach for it. I put it aside, as if saving it for an emergency—though I didn’t know what the emergency was.

Out of boredom, I looked out of the window.

That’s when I saw it…

A world full of addicts.

Not the kind you imagine when you hear the word—messy hair, scars, dark rooms. These addicts were well-dressed, sitting comfortably in cars, buses, autos. Heads bent. Fingers scrolling. Eyes glazed.

Everyone was absorbed—in texts, music, videos, games. Not present. Not aware. Just… elsewhere.

Some of them, I admit, might have been genuinely busy. But most were simply avoiding the real world—trying to feel occupied.

There was a car beside me. Seven people inside.

Two kids playing Battleground, fingers moving faster than thought. A teenage girl staring endlessly at a photograph of an actor. A mother scrolling—up, down—lost in a loop with no destination. The father watching Netflix, headphones sealing him off from the family inches away. Perhaps the grandfather recording a vlog, narrating a moment he wasn’t living.

And the driver?

The driver was texting.

Seven people. Seven screens. Not one conversation.

I felt disturbed—but also strangely superior. Observant. Awake.

That’s when the discomfort deepened.

There’s a psychological phenomenon: once you notice something, you begin to see it everywhere. Frequency illusion, they call it. Or maybe it’s just awareness arriving late.

On the way home, I saw more of them. And more.

By the time I reached my room, a thought crept in—quiet, cold, undeniable.

The chill down my spine wasn’t from what I saw outside. It was from what I realized inside.

I wasn’t just watching the addicts. I was one of them.

That realization—that moment of recognition—is where this story truly begins.

Chapter 2: The Quiet Betrayal

You cannot betray someone you do not care about. That is why the most painful betrayal is always against yourself.

Nobody warned me that self-betrayal wouldn’t feel dramatic. It arrived quietly—disguised as comfort, as rest, as “just this once.”

It usually began with a small promise.

I’ll start after this. Just five minutes more. I deserve a break today.

These weren’t lies spoken aloud. They were contracts signed silently. And the danger wasn’t that I broke them—it was that I broke them so often they stopped feeling important.

I trusted myself a little less every time I chose comfort over effort.

Discipline isn’t punishment. It’s honesty. It’s the courage to face discomfort instead of escaping it.

The real cost of distraction wasn’t time. It was identity.

I didn’t just lose hours—I lost belief in my own word. And without that belief, ambition begins to sound hollow.

The hardest person to face isn’t society. It’s the version of yourself you keep disappointing without realizing it.

The betrayal was quiet. But it was constant.

Chapter 3: When the Noise Stopped

I didn’t quit my phone. I didn’t delete apps. I simply stopped running from silence.

Silence wasn’t peaceful. It was loud.

It brought back thoughts I had avoided. But beneath that discomfort was something honest.

Boredom isn’t emptiness. It’s the mind returning to itself.

I didn’t change overnight. Awareness didn’t cure me—it gave me a choice.

Presence didn’t make life easier. It made it clearer.

This chapter isn’t about rejecting technology. It’s about reclaiming attention—the rarest currency of this century.

The noise didn’t disappear. I just stopped letting it decide who I am.
